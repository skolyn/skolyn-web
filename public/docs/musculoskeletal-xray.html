Musculoskeletal X-Ray Analysis

Fracture Detection, Joint Assessment, and Orthopedic Measurement Automation
Version: 3.0
Last Updated: February 2026
Classification: Confidential
Owner: X-Ray Engineering

1. Overview

Musculoskeletal (MSK) radiography accounts for the largest volume of X-ray examinations, encompassing all skeletal imaging from cervical spine to foot. MSK X-ray interpretation is a high-volume, repetitive task with substantial missed fracture rates, particularly in emergency departments where studies are initially interpreted by non-radiologist physicians. Published literature reports fracture miss rates of 3-9% in emergency department radiographs, with wrist, scaphoid, and hip fractures being the most commonly missed. Skolyn's MSK X-ray AI within Terbium OS provides automated fracture detection across all anatomical regions, joint space assessment, alignment measurement, and orthopedic angle computation. The module processes each study in under 5 seconds, supporting over 20 anatomical regions with region-specific analysis pipelines optimized for the unique anatomy and common pathology patterns at each body part.

2. Fracture Detection

2.1 Regional Coverage

The fracture detection engine supports 24 anatomical regions with specialized models for each: shoulder (proximal humerus, clavicle, scapula), elbow (radial head, olecranon, supracondylar), forearm (radius, ulna shaft), wrist (distal radius/ulna, scaphoid, perilunate), hand (metacarpal, phalanx), pelvis (acetabular, pubic rami, sacral), hip (femoral neck, intertrochanteric, subtrochanteric), knee (tibial plateau, patella, fibular head), ankle (lateral malleolus, medial malleolus, posterior malleolus), and foot (metatarsal, calcaneus, Lisfranc). Each regional model is trained on 5,000-15,000 annotated fracture cases with balanced representation of subtle, non-displaced fractures that are most commonly missed in clinical practice.

2.2 Fracture Classification

Detected fractures are classified by type (transverse, oblique, spiral, comminuted, avulsion, stress/insufficiency, pathologic), displacement (non-displaced, minimally displaced <2mm, displaced >2mm), and angulation (measured in degrees). For specific anatomical regions, the system applies standard orthopedic classification systems: Neer classification for proximal humerus fractures, Garden classification for femoral neck fractures, Schatzker classification for tibial plateau fractures, Lauge-Hansen for ankle fractures, and Weber classification for fibular fractures. Each classification helps guide treatment decisions regarding operative vs. conservative management.

3. Joint Assessment

The joint space analysis module quantifies joint space width at standardized measurement locations for all major joints. Joint space narrowing is graded per the Kellgren-Lawrence (KL) classification for osteoarthritis: Grade 0 (none), Grade 1 (doubtful — minute osteophyte), Grade 2 (minimal — definite osteophyte, possible narrowing), Grade 3 (moderate — moderate narrowing, sclerosis), Grade 4 (severe — near-complete loss, large osteophytes, cysts, deformity). The system measures both medial and lateral compartments for knee radiographs, detecting asymmetric narrowing patterns. For hip joints, the minimum joint space width is measured at the superior aspect per OARSI guidelines. Automated detection of osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and subchondral cysts complements the joint space measurement.

5. Implant Assessment

For post-operative radiographs, the module identifies and classifies orthopedic hardware including plates, screws, intramedullary nails, joint prostheses (total hip, total knee), and external fixation devices. The system assesses hardware position for signs of failure: screw back-out, plate fracture, periprosthetic fracture, prosthetic loosening (radiolucent lines >2mm circumferential or progressive), subsidence, and heterotopic ossification grading (Brooker classification).
