Musculoskeletal MRI Analysis

AI-Powered MSK MR Assessment: Knee, Shoulder, Spine, and Joint Analysis
Version: 3.0
Last Updated: February 2026

Owner: MRI Engineering

1. Overview

The Musculoskeletal (MSK) MRI analysis subtype covers the full range of orthopedic and sports medicine MRI examinations that constitute the highest-volume MRI workload in most radiology practices. MSK MRI represents approximately 35-40% of all MRI examinations, with knee, shoulder, and spine being the three most commonly imaged body regions. Skolyn's MSK MRI AI provides automated detection and classification of ligament tears, meniscal injuries, rotator cuff pathology, cartilage degeneration, bone marrow edema, disc herniation, and degenerative joint disease. The module processes standard clinical MSK MRI protocols (proton density fat-saturated, T1, T2 fat-saturated, STIR, and 3D cartilage sequences) with findings mapped to standardized orthopedic classification systems.

2. Knee MRI Analysis

2.1 Meniscal Assessment

The knee analysis engine provides detailed meniscal evaluation with tear detection, classification, and localization. Tears are classified by type (horizontal, vertical/longitudinal, radial, complex, bucket-handle, root), grade (I — intrasubstance degeneration without surface extension, II — linear signal reaching one articular surface, III — signal extending to both surfaces or displaced fragment), and location (anterior horn, body, posterior horn for both medial and lateral menisci). Bucket-handle tears are specifically flagged due to their surgical urgency, with automated detection of the displaced fragment, double PCL sign, and absent bow-tie sign on sagittal images. The meniscal assessment achieves 94.1% sensitivity and 92.7% specificity, validated on 12,000 clinical knee MRI studies with arthroscopic correlation.

2.2 Ligament Evaluation

All four major knee ligaments (ACL, PCL, MCL, LCL) are evaluated for integrity. ACL assessment includes classification as intact, partial tear (with fiber involvement percentage and location — proximal, mid-substance, or distal), or complete tear, with secondary signs including anterior tibial translation, posterior femoral condyle bone bruise pattern, and Segond fracture detection. PCL evaluation includes classification by grade (I-III) and identification of associated posterolateral corner injuries. MCL assessment follows the O'Donoghue classification system, and the system detects the clinically important "unhappy triad" combination (ACL + MCL + medial meniscal tear) that alters surgical planning.

2.3 Cartilage Grading

Articular cartilage is evaluated across all femoral condyle, tibial plateau, patellar, and trochlear surfaces using the modified Outerbridge classification (Grade 0 — normal, Grade I — signal changes without surface defect, Grade II — partial-thickness defect <50%, Grade III — partial-thickness defect >50%, Grade IV — full-thickness defect with subchondral bone exposure). Cartilage analysis uses 3D spoiled gradient echo (SPGR) or DESS sequences when available, with quantitative thickness mapping and comparison to age-matched normative data.

3. Shoulder MRI Analysis

The shoulder analysis engine evaluates the rotator cuff (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis), labrum, biceps tendon, and acromioclavicular joint. Rotator cuff tear assessment includes classification by type (partial-thickness — articular-sided, bursal-sided, or interstitial; full-thickness; or massive), tear size measurement (AP length and retraction distance), muscle quality assessment (Goutallier fatty infiltration grading 0-4), and tendon retraction staging (Patte classification). For partial tears, thickness involvement percentage is reported. Labral assessment covers SLAP tear typing (I-X), Bankart lesion detection (osseous and soft tissue variants), and posterior labral tear identification. The module also measures the critical shoulder angle (CSA) and acromion morphology (Bigliani type I-III) for biomechanical risk assessment.

4. Spine MRI Analysis

Spine MRI analysis covers cervical, thoracic, and lumbar spine with level-by-level assessment. For each intervertebral disc, the system evaluates Pfirrmann degeneration grade (I-V), disc contour (normal, bulge, protrusion, extrusion, sequestration), and neural foraminal and central canal stenosis severity (mild, moderate, severe). Disc measurements include disc height, protrusion size, and canal diameter at each level. Vertebral body assessment includes Modic endplate changes (types I-III), compression fracture detection with height loss quantification, and identification of aggressive marrow replacement patterns suggestive of metastatic disease. Facet joint arthropathy is graded bilaterally at each level (grades 0-3), and ligamentum flavum hypertrophy is measured.
