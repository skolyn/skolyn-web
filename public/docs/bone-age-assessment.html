Bone Age Assessment

Automated Skeletal Maturity Assessment: Greulich-Pyle and Tanner-Whitehouse Methods with Adult Height Prediction
Version: 3.0
Last Updated: February 2026
Classification: Confidential
Owner: X-Ray Engineering

1. Overview

Bone age assessment is a fundamental tool in pediatric radiology and endocrinology, used to evaluate skeletal maturity by comparing a child's ossification pattern to age-and-sex-specific reference standards. Bone age determination is essential for evaluating growth disorders (short stature, precocious puberty, delayed puberty), monitoring response to growth hormone therapy, predicting adult height, and guiding surgical timing for orthopedic procedures such as epiphysiodesis. The standard examination involves a single left hand and wrist radiograph, which is then compared against reference atlases. Traditional manual bone age assessment is time-consuming (averaging 5-10 minutes per study), subject to significant inter-reader variability (standard deviation of 0.55-0.82 years between expert readers), and influenced by reader experience and fatigue. Skolyn's bone age AI within Terbium OS automates both the Greulich-Pyle atlas and Tanner-Whitehouse 3 (TW3) methods, delivering results in under 5 seconds with precision that exceeds average inter-reader agreement.

2. Greulich-Pyle Method

The Greulich-Pyle (GP) method compares the overall appearance of the hand and wrist radiograph to a reference atlas of standard images representing specific bone ages. Skolyn's AI implements a deep learning atlas matching approach: the input radiograph is processed through a convolutional neural network (InceptionResNet-V2) that produces a continuous bone age prediction by learning the mapping from radiographic appearance to bone age directly, rather than discrete atlas matching. The GP model is trained on the RSNA Pediatric Bone Age dataset (12,600 annotated hand radiographs) augmented with an additional 8,000 internally annotated studies representing diverse ethnic populations (critical because the original GP atlas was derived exclusively from Caucasian children of European descent in the 1930s). The model outputs bone age in months with 95% confidence intervals based on prediction uncertainty estimation.

3. Tanner-Whitehouse 3 (TW3) Method

3.1 Individual Bone Scoring

The TW3 method evaluates skeletal maturity by independently staging specific bones of the hand and wrist at discrete maturity stages. Skolyn's AI segments and individually classifies 13 specific regions of interest (ROIs): radius, ulna, first metacarpal, third metacarpal, fifth metacarpal, proximal phalanx of the first finger, middle phalanx of the third finger, middle phalanx of the fifth finger, distal phalanx of the first finger, distal phalanx of the third finger, distal phalanx of the fifth finger, capitate, and hamate. Each ROI is classified into its appropriate maturity stage (A through I, with variable number of stages per bone), with maturity scores assigned per the TW3 scoring system. The weighted sum of individual bone maturity scores produces the composite RUS (Radius, Ulna, Short bones) score, which is converted to bone age using the standard TW3 lookup tables by sex.

3.2 ROI Segmentation Quality

Accurate TW3 scoring requires precise segmentation of individual ossification centers. The ROI segmentation module uses a two-stage approach: first, a hand landmark detection network identifies the anatomical positions of all 13 ROI centers; second, a region-specific segmentation network extracts high-resolution patches around each center for detailed staging analysis. This approach handles the wide range of appearances from infantile (few ossification centers visible) through adolescent (near-complete ossification with epiphyseal fusion) with a single unified pipeline. Image quality assessment validates that each ROI is adequately visualized before staging, flagging cases where positioning artifacts (rotation, overlap) may compromise individual bone scoring.

4. Adult Height Prediction

The module integrates bone age with patient demographics (current height, weight, sex, mid-parental height) to predict adult height using the Bayley-Pinneau method. The Bayley-Pinneau tables predict adult height as a percentage of current height based on the relationship between bone age and chronological age. Advanced bone age (bone age exceeding chronological age) predicts earlier epiphyseal fusion and shorter adult height relative to current percentile, while delayed bone age predicts later fusion and potentially taller adult height. The prediction includes confidence intervals to communicate the inherent uncertainty in adult height estimation.
