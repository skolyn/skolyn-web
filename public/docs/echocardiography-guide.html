1. Overview

Echocardiography is the most widely performed cardiac imaging modality, with over 30 million studies performed annually in the United States. However, echocardiographic interpretation is highly operator-dependent, time-consuming, and subject to significant inter-observer variability — studies report 10-15% variability in ejection fraction measurement between expert readers. Skolyn's echocardiography AI within Scandium OS automates the entire echocardiographic analysis workflow, from view identification through chamber quantification, wall motion analysis, valve assessment, and strain imaging. The module processes standard transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) studies with apical 4-chamber, apical 2-chamber, parasternal long-axis (PLAX), and parasternal short-axis (PSAX) cine clips, delivering comprehensive results in under 45 seconds with reproducibility that exceeds expert inter-reader agreement.

2. View Classification

The first stage of the echocardiography pipeline is automated view identification. Each acquired cine clip is classified into one of 23 standard echocardiographic views using an EfficientNet-B5 classifier trained on 2 million annotated frames from 150,000 studies. Supported views include apical 4-chamber (A4C), apical 2-chamber (A2C), apical 3-chamber (A3C), apical 5-chamber (A5C), parasternal long-axis (PLAX), parasternal short-axis at aortic valve, mitral valve, and papillary muscle levels, subcostal 4-chamber, subcostal IVC, suprasternal notch, and all standard Doppler views (PW, CW, color, tissue Doppler). View classification accuracy exceeds 97% across all views, enabling fully automated study routing without manual view labeling.

3. Chamber Quantification

3.1 Left Ventricular Function

LV ejection fraction is computed using both the modified Simpson's biplane method (automated endocardial tracing in A4C and A2C views at end-diastole and end-systole) and a direct deep learning regression model for robustness. The biplane method uses a U-Net segmentation network with temporal attention that identifies end-diastolic and end-systolic phases automatically and segments the LV endocardium with sub-pixel precision. The regression model provides an independent EF estimate from the apical views, and the final reported EF is the consensus of both approaches. Additional LV measurements include end-diastolic volume (EDV), end-systolic volume (ESV), stroke volume (SV), and cardiac output (CO), all indexed to body surface area (BSA) and compared to ASE-recommended normal ranges by age and sex.

4. Wall Motion Analysis

Wall motion is assessed using the AHA 17-segment model with automated classification of each segment as normokinetic (score 1), hypokinetic (score 2), akinetic (score 3), or dyskinetic (score 4). The wall motion score index (WMSI) provides a global summary of contractile function (normal WMSI = 1.0). Regional wall motion abnormalities are mapped to coronary artery territories for clinical correlation. The AI achieves 89% agreement with expert consensus across all 17 segments, compared to 82% inter-reader agreement between expert echocardiographers.

5. Strain Analysis

Global longitudinal strain (GLS) is computed using AI-based speckle tracking across the A4C, A2C, and A3C views. The algorithm tracks myocardial deformation patterns frame-by-frame, generating strain curves for each of the 17 LV segments. Normal GLS ranges from -18% to -22% (more negative = better function). GLS is clinically superior to LVEF for early detection of subclinical systolic dysfunction, particularly in oncology patients receiving cardiotoxic chemotherapy (trastuzumab, anthracyclines) and in hypertensive heart disease. The module generates the standard 17-segment bull's-eye strain map with red-blue color encoding.
