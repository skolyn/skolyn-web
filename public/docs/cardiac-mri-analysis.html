Cardiac MRI Analysis

Comprehensive Cardiac MR Assessment: Ventricular Function, Tissue Characterization, and Myocardial Mapping

1. Overview

The Cardiac MRI analysis subtype provides fully automated assessment of cardiac structure and function from cine SSFP (Steady-State Free Precession), late gadolinium enhancement (LGE), and parametric mapping sequences. Cardiac MRI is considered the gold standard for ventricular volume and function assessment, myocardial tissue characterization, and viability evaluation. Skolyn's cardiac MRI AI automates the labor-intensive manual contouring process, reduces inter-observer variability, and provides comprehensive quantitative analysis in under 30 seconds — compared to the 20-45 minutes typically required for manual analysis. The module supports both standard and non-standard short-axis stack orientations, automatically detecting and correcting for through-plane motion artifacts.

2. Ventricular Function Analysis

2.1 Automated Contouring

The automated contouring engine segments left ventricular (LV) endocardial and epicardial borders, and right ventricular (RV) endocardial borders across all cardiac phases in the short-axis cine stack. The segmentation model (nnU-Net with temporal attention) identifies end-diastolic (ED) and end-systolic (ES) phases automatically from the cine loop, then propagates contours across all phases using a temporal consistency constraint that ensures physiologically plausible wall motion. Papillary muscles and trabeculations are handled using a dual-threshold approach: included in myocardial mass for mass calculations and excluded from cavity volume for ejection fraction calculations, following SCMR (Society for Cardiovascular Magnetic Resonance) guidelines.

3. Wall Motion Analysis

Automated wall motion assessment follows the AHA 17-segment model, classifying each segment as normokinetic, hypokinetic, akinetic, or dyskinetic based on endocardial excursion and wall thickening measurements. The wall motion score index (WMSI) is computed as the sum of segment scores divided by 17. Regional wall motion abnormalities are mapped to coronary vascular territories (LAD, LCx, RCA) for correlation with coronary artery disease distribution. The system also detects and characterizes specific motion patterns including paradoxical septal motion (post-surgical, RV volume overload), dyssynchrony (potential CRT candidates), and apical ballooning (Takotsubo pattern).

4. Late Gadolinium Enhancement

4.1 Scar Quantification

The LGE analysis module performs automated scar detection and quantification using a combination of signal intensity thresholding (6-SD, 5-SD, and FWHM methods) and deep learning-based segmentation. Scar is mapped to the AHA 17-segment model with transmurality grading (subendocardial <25%, 25-50%, 50-75%, and transmural >75%) for viability assessment. Total scar burden is reported as a percentage of LV myocardial mass. Enhancement patterns are classified as ischemic (subendocardial following a coronary territory) or non-ischemic (mid-wall, epicardial, patchy/diffuse), supporting differential diagnosis of cardiomyopathy etiology — a critical distinction that directs patient management toward revascularization versus medical therapy.

5. Parametric Mapping

The module processes native T1 maps, post-contrast T1 maps, T2 maps, and ECV (extracellular volume) maps with automated myocardial segmentation and per-segment value extraction. Regional mapping values are compared against sequence-specific normative databases (MOLLI, ShMOLLI, SASHA for T1; T2-prep bSSFP for T2). Elevated native T1 and T2 values suggest myocardial edema/inflammation (myocarditis, acute MI), while elevated ECV indicates diffuse fibrosis (HCM, DCM, amyloidosis). The mapping analysis is particularly valuable for detecting diffuse myocardial disease processes that may not be visible on LGE imaging.
